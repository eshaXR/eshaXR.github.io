Can Your Headphones Protect You?

Assessing the effectiveness of vehicle-to-pedestrian alert signals delivered through personal listening devices in an immersive VR crosswalk environment — with silent electric vehicles.

Participants N = 31
Conditions 2×2×2×3 factorial
Platform Unreal Engine + Meta Quest 2
IRB Approved #23-1188
01 — Overview

The Problem

🔇

Silent EVs, Hidden Danger

Electric vehicles emit just 48–56 dB(A) vs. 61–70 dB(A) for combustion engines — removing the acoustic cues pedestrians rely on to cross safely.

🎧

PLDs as a Double-Edged Tool

Over 1 in 3 pedestrians actively use a device while crossing. Headphones can either mask environmental cues — or become the alert system itself.

⚡

Reaction Time Is Everything

A vehicle at 30 mph covers 13.5 meters per second. Even a 1-second improvement in alert reaction time can prevent a fatality.

🌐

V2P Communication

Vehicle-to-pedestrian systems can route safety alerts directly to wearables. This study tests which signal designs work best under distraction.

05 — Key Findings & Implications

What This Means

01

Open-Ear Devices Win on Performance

AC open-ear (AirPods) and BC open-ear (AfterShokz) produced significantly faster crossing times than AC closed-ear headphones. The mean difference of ~74ms was statistically significant (p < .001, η²ₚ = .333) — enough to matter in real traffic at 30 mph.

02

Verbal Alerts Are Clearer and More Trusted

Participants not only performed better with verbal alerts under most conditions — they rated them higher for clarity, satisfaction, and trustworthiness. ~72% preferred verbal signals in the post-study survey. Actionable language ("You can cross now") reduces ambiguity under stress.

03

Closed-Ear Devices Create a Hidden Hazard

Sony WH-1000XM4 (ANC over-ear) showed the largest performance outlier: 1.29 seconds crossing time with M2 verbal signals in visual conditions — 65% longer than the study mean. Noise cancellation occludes critical ambient cues even when alerts play directly through the device.

04

Signaling Method × PLD Interaction Is Critical

The interaction between signaling method and device type was the strongest effect in the study (η²ₚ = .565). M2 ("safe gap") alerts backfired with closed-ear devices — suggesting that any real-world V2P deployment must account for the user's device type, not just the signal design.

05

Nighttime Doesn't Hurt When the Alert Is Right

The visual environment (day vs. night) alone did not significantly affect trigger reaction time. This suggests well-designed PLD alerts can compensate for poor visibility — a key finding for campus safety infrastructure where nighttime crossings are common.

🏙️

Smart City V2P Systems

Results directly inform alert signal design for connected vehicle systems: use verbal, actionable signals routed through open-ear wearables.

🦺

Campus Safety

Virginia Tech crosswalk environments mirror the study scenario. PLD-based alerts could augment or replace traditional signal infrastructure.

♿

Accessibility

Open-ear bone conduction devices offer both safety benefits and accessibility for users who need environmental awareness at all times.

📱

Wearable Tech Design

Manufacturers should consider the safety tradeoffs of high isolation ANC in AirPod-style and over-ear form factors for pedestrian use cases.
